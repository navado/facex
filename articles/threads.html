My first attempt ran at 24 milliseconds. The goal was under 4. I was six times too slow.

So I did what engineers do — I measured every single operation inside the neural network. There are about 300 of them. Matrix multiplications, normalizations, activation functions, memory copies.

Everyone on the internet said "optimize the matrix multiplication, that's where all the time goes in neural networks."

I measured. Matrix multiplication was 6 percent of total time. Six.

The slow parts were the boring stuff nobody writes papers about. Computing averages. Converting memory layouts. Calculating activation functions one number at a time.

I rewrote every single one using processor instructions that work on 8 or 16 numbers simultaneously. Took six months. Got to 3 milliseconds.

The answer was where nobody was looking.

Making my engine fast was hard. Making it accurate was harder.

After the speed optimizations, my engine looked at a face and produced 512 numbers. The reference model produced 512 different numbers. They should have been identical.

The similarity between them was 0.067 out of 1.0. Basically random noise.

I spent three weeks comparing every single number at every single layer — 286 checkpoints — between my code and a reference implementation. Found seven bugs. Each one tiny. Each one invisible. Together they turned signal into garbage.

The most devious: a part of the network that looked like it depended on the input image actually didn't. It was a constant. I was recomputing it every time, and tiny rounding errors accumulated through 17 layers until the output was meaningless.

Seven fixes later: 1.000 similarity. Exact match.

The first 16 milliseconds of optimization took me two weeks. I felt unstoppable. Every morning I'd find a new bottleneck, write a new SIMD kernel, and shave off another millisecond.

Then I hit 5 milliseconds and everything stopped. Tried assembly language. Tried JIT compilation. Tried different compilers. Tried threading. Tried operation fusion. Four months of trying.

Then I discovered my CPU had a wider instruction set I wasn't using. AVX-512. Nobody told me. I had to read the processor flags myself.

Rewrote the hottest kernels. 5 became 3.

Two lessons: first, the wall isn't always real — sometimes you're just not seeing the door. Second, read your CPU's spec sheet. It knows things your compiler doesn't.
