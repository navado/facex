The Sweetest Millisecond

How a developer from Kazakhstan outran Microsoft — and went to buy a pastry.

···

The bakery is seven minutes from my apartment. I know this because I've been measuring everything in milliseconds for the past six months, and some of that sickness has leaked into the rest of my life.

But today I'm not thinking about milliseconds. Today I'm buying a sweet roll. Because my brain earned it.

Let me tell you why.

···

Six months ago I opened a blank file on my computer and typed the first line of C code. I was going to write a face recognition engine. Not use one — write one. From scratch. By hand. Every multiplication, every addition, every memory access.

The neural network I chose was small. EdgeFace-XS — one-point-seven-seven million parameters. For comparison, ChatGPT has over a trillion. This little thing was supposed to look at a photo of a face and turn it into a list of 512 numbers. Compare two lists — and you know if it's the same person.

Simple in theory. Everyone uses a framework for this. You install Python, download ONNX Runtime — Microsoft's neural network engine — feed it the model, and it just works. Three-point-nine milliseconds per face on my CPU. Fast enough for anyone.

I wanted to do it without the framework. Without Python. Without Microsoft. Just me and the processor.

My first version ran in twenty-four milliseconds. Six times slower than the thing I was trying to replace.

Not a great start.

···

I did what any engineer would do. I profiled. I measured every single one of the three hundred operations inside the neural network. Timed each one. Made a spreadsheet. Sorted by time.

And here's where the story gets interesting.

If you ask any machine learning engineer what's slow in a neural network, they'll say matrix multiplication. It's the big operation. The heavy lifting. The thing that GPUs were invented for.

I looked at my spreadsheet.

6%

Matrix multiplication's share of total inference time.

Six percent. The "heavy lifting" was barely lifting at all.

The real killers were the operations nobody writes blog posts about. Layer normalization — computing the mean and standard deviation of a row of numbers, seventeen times per image. GELU activation — a mathematical function applied to every single number in the network, also seventeen times. And memory layout conversions — reshuffling data from one format to another, back and forth, on every layer.

The glue. The boring stuff between the important parts.

Except in this network, the glue was the important part.

···

So I rewrote the glue.

Modern CPUs have special instructions that can process eight or sixteen numbers at once. They're called SIMD — Single Instruction, Multiple Data. Intel calls their version AVX2. Instead of adding one number to another, you add eight pairs simultaneously. Instead of computing one square root, you compute eight.

I wrote custom SIMD kernels for every operation. Layer normalization that computes mean and variance in a single pass instead of two. A GELU function built from a polynomial approximation of the error function — an equation from a 1964 math textbook, translated into CPU instructions that process eight floating-point numbers per clock cycle.

Twenty-four milliseconds became eight.

Then I tackled the matrix multiplications. Even at six percent, they were worth optimizing. I quantized the weights to 8-bit integers and wrote a microkernel using an instruction called vpmaddubsw that multiplies sixteen pairs of integers and adds them up in one cycle. Discovered a saturation bug — when the partial sums exceeded 32,767, the hardware silently clipped them, corrupting the result. Spent two weeks debugging that.

Eight became five.

···

And then the wall.

Five milliseconds. No matter what I did. I tried writing in assembly language. I tried JIT-compiling custom kernels at startup. I tried three different compilers. I tried link-time optimization, profile-guided optimization, thread pool tuning, operation fusion.

Nothing. Four months of nothing.

I started reading academic papers. Dozens of them. Looked through my old research files. Read Intel's optimization manual — all four thousand pages.

And then, on a random Tuesday, buried in the output of a CPU diagnostic tool, I saw it.

My processor supported AVX-512.

AVX-512 is a wider version of the SIMD instructions I was already using. Instead of processing eight numbers at once, it processes sixteen. Not every CPU has it. Nobody had told me mine did. I never thought to check.

I rewrote the hottest kernels. Sixteen numbers at a time instead of eight. New instructions that do integer dot products without the saturation bug.

3.0 ms

Final inference time. ONNX Runtime: 3.9 ms.

I stared at the number on my screen. Ran the benchmark again. And again. Same result.

I was faster than Microsoft.

···

But speed was the easy part. The hard part — the part that nearly broke me — was accuracy.

A face recognition engine that's fast but wrong is worse than useless. It's dangerous. It would tell you strangers are your friends and your friends are strangers.

After my speed optimizations, I compared my engine's output to the reference. The cosine similarity — a measure of how similar two lists of numbers are, where 1.0 means identical — was 0.067.

Zero-point-zero-six-seven. My engine was producing random noise.

I spent three weeks on this. I dumped every intermediate tensor — all 286 of them — from both my engine and a Python reference implementation. Compared them number by number, layer by layer, dimension by dimension.

Found seven bugs.

The first was embarrassing — I used the wrong weight index in one block. W(38) instead of W(39). One digit. The bias instead of the gamma parameter.

The last was devious. Part of the neural network computes something called a position embedding — it tells the network where each patch of the image is located. In the ONNX graph, it looked like a convolution applied to the input. So I convolved the input.

But the input to that convolution wasn't actually the input. It was a constant. A pre-computed tensor baked into the model at training time. It never changes. I was computing it fresh every frame, using slightly different floating-point arithmetic, and the tiny rounding errors accumulated through seventeen layers until the output was meaningless.

Each bug fix added about 0.1 to the cosine similarity. Seven bugs. 0.067 became 1.000.

Exact match.

···

So here's what I have now.

A face recognition engine written in four thousand lines of C. It takes a 112-by-112 pixel photo of a face and produces a 512-dimensional embedding vector in three milliseconds. It's 23 percent faster than Microsoft's ONNX Runtime. The library is 148 kilobytes. The total deployment — binary plus model weights — is seven megabytes. You can copy it to any Linux server and run it. No Python. No Docker. No package manager. No dependencies of any kind.

99.73 percent accuracy on the standard LFW benchmark. Same as models sixty times larger.

I built it in Kazakhstan. By myself. With a free compiler and a consumer CPU.

It's open source now. Apache 2.0 license. Anyone can use it.

···

I'm putting on my jacket. The bakery closes in forty minutes, and I've been staring at benchmark numbers all morning.

There's a specific pastry I like — a round one with custard filling and powdered sugar on top. It costs about a dollar. I've been eating one every time I hit a milestone. After the first SIMD kernel worked. After the INT8 bug was fixed. After cosine similarity hit 1.0.

Today's pastry is for the final number. Three-point-zero milliseconds. Faster than Microsoft.

Six months of work. Seven bugs. Forty-nine SIMD kernels. Four thousand lines of code. One pastry.

Sometimes the best way to celebrate beating a trillion-dollar company is a one-dollar sweet roll.

The bakery is seven minutes away. Four hundred and twenty thousand milliseconds. I used to think that was fast.

Now I know what fast really means.
